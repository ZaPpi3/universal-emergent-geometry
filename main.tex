\title{Universal Emergent Geometry from Mutual Information Across Quantum Models}

\author{Paul Jarvis}
\affiliation{Independent Researcher, United Kingdom}
\email{mrpaulwjarvis@gmail.com}

\date{\today}

\begin{abstract}
We demonstrate that mutual-information--induced graph Laplacians provide a universal geometric
diagnostic of quantum phase structure across multiple models of quantum matter. Using exact
diagonalization for system sizes $N = 6, 8, 10, 12$, we compute mutual-information matrices from
ground states of the XXZ chain, the transverse-field Ising model (TFIM), a classical spin-glass
Hamiltonian, and free fermions, and extract geometric invariants from the normalized Laplacian
spectrum.

Across the quantum models, we find two independent universality classes of entanglement-induced
geometry: (1) the XXZ family, which includes the free-fermion point at $\Delta = 0$ and exhibits
continuous critical or integrable behaviour depending on parameters; and (2) the TFIM, which shows
sharp symmetry-breaking geometry near its transition. The classical spin-glass model produces
identically zero mutual information, yielding a trivial Laplacian spectrum and serving as a control
case demonstrating that the MI-Laplacian pipeline does not generate spurious geometric structure
when correlations are absent. Extending the finite-size analysis to $N=12$ confirms that all
signatures persist smoothly, with no qualitative change in behaviour across the accessible range.

Pushing the two critical points further via density matrix renormalization group (DMRG) to
$N=20$ reveals a genuine quantitative distinction between the two universality classes: the TFIM
(Ising, $c=1/2$) critical point follows a clean power law with stable exponent $b \approx 2.10$
across the full range, while the XXZ (Heisenberg, $c=1$) critical point is not well described by
a power law at all -- its residuals show a clear systematic pattern -- but is fit almost exactly
by a logarithmically corrected form, the known finite-size signature of the marginally irrelevant
operator present at the SU(2)-symmetric Heisenberg point and absent at the Ising point. This is
reported as an empirical finding rather than a theoretical derivation.

All numerical data and figures are reproducible from the release dataset accompanying this
manuscript.
\end{abstract}

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\section{Introduction}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

The hypothesis that geometry may emerge from quantum information has gained traction across
quantum gravity, condensed matter physics, and quantum information science. Mutual information
(MI) provides a natural measure of correlation structure, and graph Laplacians constructed from MI
have been proposed as discrete analogues of Laplace--Beltrami operators on emergent manifolds.

A central open question is whether MI-induced geometry behaves \emph{universally} across different
quantum models. To address this, one requires (i) multiple Hamiltonians with distinct phase
structures, (ii) finite-size scaling, (iii) reproducible numerical diagnostics, and (iv) geometric
invariants that respond meaningfully to entanglement structure.

This work provides a dataset satisfying these criteria for two independent quantum universality
classes—the XXZ/free-fermion family and the transverse-field Ising model—together with a classical
spin-glass control model. Using exact diagonalization, we compute MI matrices and extract geometric
invariants from the normalized Laplacian spectrum across system sizes $N = 6, 8, 10, 12$. We then
extend the two critical points beyond exact diagonalization's reach using DMRG, up to $N=20$, and
show that this reveals a genuine quantitative distinction between the two universality classes that
is invisible at smaller sizes.

\section{Models and Methods}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

We study four representative Hamiltonians:

\subsection{XXZ Chain}

\begin{equation}
H_{\mathrm{XXZ}} = \sum_{i=1}^{N-1}
\left(
S_i^x S_{i+1}^x +
S_i^y S_{i+1}^y +
\Delta S_i^z S_{i+1}^z
\right),
\end{equation}
which exhibits a continuous SU(2)-symmetric critical point at $\Delta = 1$.

\subsection{Transverse-Field Ising Model (TFIM)}

\begin{equation}
H_{\mathrm{TFIM}} =
- \sum_{i=1}^{N-1} \sigma_i^z \sigma_{i+1}^z
- h \sum_{i=1}^{N} \sigma_i^x,
\end{equation}
with a symmetry-breaking transition near $h = 1$.

\subsection{Classical Spin Glass}

\begin{equation}
H_{\mathrm{SG}} = \sum_{i<j} J_{ij} \sigma_i^z \sigma_j^z,
\end{equation}

where $J_{ij}$ are Gaussian random couplings. As written, this model is \emph{classical}: it contains
no transverse field or off-diagonal terms. Consequently, its ground state is always a product state
with no entanglement. The mutual-information matrix therefore satisfies $I_{ij}=0$ for all pairs
$(i,j)$, independent of disorder strength. No disorder averaging was performed; however, for this
classical model, averaging would not change the result because $I_{ij}$ is identically zero for every
realization.

Because $I_{ij}=0$, the normalized Laplacian reduces to the identity matrix, yielding
$\lambda_1 = \lambda_2 = 1$ and $\Delta_{\mathrm{gap}} = 0$ for all disorder strengths. The MI decay
exponent $\alpha$ is undefined. This model therefore serves as a \emph{control case} demonstrating
that the pipeline does not generate spurious geometric structure when correlations are absent.

We note that $H_{\mathrm{SG}}$ is invariant under a global spin flip ($\sigma_i^z \to -\sigma_i^z$
for all $i$ simultaneously), so its ground state is always at least doubly degenerate. A generic
eigensolver handed a degenerate subspace can return an arbitrary superposition of basis states
rather than a pure computational basis state, and such a superposition can carry spurious nonzero
entanglement even though the underlying Hamiltonian is exactly diagonal. Ground states for this
model were therefore obtained by direct minimization over the diagonal of $H_{\mathrm{SG}}$ rather
than via a generic eigensolver, which is guaranteed correct by construction.

\subsection{Free Fermions (XX Chain)}

\begin{equation}
H_{\mathrm{FF}} =
- t \sum_{i=1}^{N-1}
\left(
\sigma_i^x \sigma_{i+1}^x +
\sigma_i^y \sigma_{i+1}^y
\right),
\end{equation}
an integrable model with scale-free entanglement.

Ground states are obtained via exact diagonalization. The mutual-information matrix is computed as
\begin{equation}
I_{ij} = S_i + S_j - S_{ij},
\end{equation}
where $S_i$ and $S_{ij}$ are von Neumann entropies of reduced density matrices.

The normalized Laplacian is
\begin{equation}
L = \mathbb{I} - D^{-1/2} I D^{-1/2},
\end{equation}
with $D$ the degree matrix. We extract:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\lambda_1$: lowest nonzero eigenvalue,
    \item $\lambda_2$: second nonzero eigenvalue,
    \item $\Delta_{\mathrm{gap}} = \lambda_2 - \lambda_1$,
    \item $\alpha$: MI decay exponent from $I(r) \sim r^{-\alpha}$ (when defined).
\end{itemize}

Spectral embeddings are obtained from the eigenvectors $v_1, v_2$ corresponding to $\lambda_1,
\lambda_2$.

\section{Finite-Size Scaling}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

To verify that the geometric signatures persist under finite-size scaling, we extract $\lambda_1$ at
key parameter values for $N = 6, 8, 10, 12$. The XXZ/free-fermion equivalence is visible at
$\Delta = 0$ for every size, and the TFIM symmetry-breaking signature strengthens smoothly and
monotonically as $N$ increases.

The table and Fig.~\ref{fig:finite_size_scaling} together confirm:

\begin{itemize}
    \item XXZ and free fermions share identical $\lambda_1$ at $\Delta = 0$ for all sizes,
    including the newly added $N=12$ point.
    \item The XXZ critical point shows consistent, monotonically decreasing finite-size behaviour.
    \item The TFIM symmetry-breaking signature strengthens with increasing $N$, continuing to
    decrease from $N=10$ to $N=12$ with no sign of saturation.
    \item The spin-glass model remains exactly trivial ($\lambda_1 = 1$) for all sizes, as required
    by the exact diagonal structure of $H_{\mathrm{SG}}$.
\end{itemize}

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\section{Extended Critical-Point Scaling via DMRG}
\label{sec:dmrg}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

Exact diagonalization limits the accessible system size to $N \lesssim 12$--$14$ in practice. To
test whether the qualitative differences between the two critical points reflect a genuine,
quantitatively distinguishable difference in universality class, we extend both critical points
--- XXZ at $\Delta=1$ and TFIM at $h=1$ --- to $N=20$ using the density matrix renormalization
group (DMRG), implemented via the \texttt{quimb} tensor-network library. The DMRG ground states and
resulting mutual-information matrices were validated against this work's own exact-diagonalization
values at $N=8$ before being trusted for larger sizes, matching to six decimal places.

\subsection{Two Qualitatively Different Scaling Laws}

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{release/critical_scaling_extended.png}
    \caption{
    \textbf{DMRG-extended critical-point scaling, $N=6$ to $20$.}
    Top row: $\lambda_1(N)$ at the TFIM critical point (left) and XXZ critical point (right), with
    fitted curves overlaid. Bottom row: fit residuals. The TFIM residuals are small and show no
    systematic trend, consistent with a genuine power law. The XXZ power-law residuals (gray,
    dashed) trace a clear systematic arc --- the signature of an incorrect functional form ---
    while the log-corrected residuals (blue) remain flat and near zero across the full range, an
    order of magnitude smaller than the power-law residuals.
    }
    \label{fig:critical_scaling}
\end{figure}

We fit $\lambda_1(N)$ at each critical point to two candidate forms: a pure power law,
\begin{equation}
\lambda_1(N) = a\,N^{-b},
\end{equation}
and a logarithmically corrected form,
\begin{equation}
\lambda_1(N) = \frac{a}{N}\left(1 + \frac{c}{\ln N}\right),
\end{equation}
the latter being the known finite-size signature of a marginally irrelevant operator.

\textbf{TFIM (Ising, $c=1/2$).} The pure power law fits the data essentially exactly
($b = 2.098$, $R^2 = 0.999999$), with residuals that are small in absolute magnitude and show no
systematic trend across the fitted range (Fig.~\ref{fig:critical_scaling}, bottom left). This is
consistent with the Ising CFT, which has no marginally irrelevant operator at this point.

\textbf{XXZ (Heisenberg, $c=1$).} The pure power law does \emph{not} describe the data well
despite a superficially high $R^2$ ($b = 0.891$, $R^2 = 0.999671$): its residuals trace a clear
systematic arc across the fitted range --- negative, rising to a peak, then falling again --- which
is the signature of a missing term in the fit function rather than statistical noise. The
log-corrected form removes this pattern almost entirely, improving $R^2$ to $0.999999$ with
residuals at the noise floor and roughly an order of magnitude smaller than the power-law
residuals (Fig.~\ref{fig:critical_scaling}, bottom right).

\subsection{Interpretation}

The functional form that fits the XXZ critical point is the same form expected on general grounds
at the SU(2)-symmetric Heisenberg point, which is known to host a marginally irrelevant operator
that generates exactly this kind of logarithmic finite-size correction; the Ising critical point,
which has no such operator, shows no such correction. We emphasize that this is reported as an
\emph{empirical} finding: the data are well described by the log-corrected functional form, and
that functional form is the one associated in the literature with a marginal operator at this
specific critical point, but no derivation is offered here connecting the graph-Laplacian
observable $\lambda_1$ to the underlying operator content of the CFT. Establishing such a
connection --- i.e., deriving \emph{why} this particular spectral observable should be sensitive to
a marginal operator in the first place --- is left for future work.

\section{Discussion}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

The quantum models studied here fall into two independent universality classes of
entanglement-induced geometry. The XXZ chain and the free-fermion model form a single family:
their ground-state mutual-information matrices differ only at floating-point precision, reflecting
the fact that the free-fermion Hamiltonian is entanglement-equivalent to the XXZ chain at
$\Delta = 0$. This family exhibits continuous critical geometry at $\Delta = 1$ and integrable flat
geometry at $\Delta = 0$. The transverse-field Ising model constitutes the second universality
class, showing sharp symmetry-breaking geometry near its transition.

The classical spin-glass model produces no entanglement and therefore no mutual information. Its
geometric invariants are trivial, and its plotted $\alpha$ values are numerical placeholders. This
behaviour is not a physical signature of a quantum spin glass; rather, it serves as a control case
demonstrating that the MI-Laplacian pipeline does not generate spurious geometric structure when
correlations are absent.

Extending the accessible system size from $N=10$ to $N=12$ via exact diagonalization provides an
independent check on all of the above: every qualitative signature persists without modification.
Pushing further to $N=20$ via DMRG (Sec.~\ref{sec:dmrg}) goes beyond a qualitative check and
provides a genuine quantitative distinction between the two universality classes: the TFIM critical
point obeys a clean power law throughout, while the XXZ critical point requires a log-corrected
form whose structure matches the known signature of the Heisenberg point's marginal operator. This
is, to our knowledge, the first demonstration that an MI-Laplacian spectral observable is sensitive
to this specific, subtle piece of established CFT structure, and it substantially strengthens the
claim that the two universality classes identified here are not merely qualitatively different but
quantitatively distinguishable in a way that connects to known physics.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\section{Conclusion}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

Mutual-information Laplacians provide a universal geometric diagnostic of quantum phase structure.
The classical spin-glass model acts as a useful control case, while the two independent quantum
universality classes—the XXZ/free-fermion family and the TFIM—exhibit clear and reproducible
geometric signatures of their respective phase structures. Finite-size scaling confirms the
robustness of these signatures across $N = 6, 8, 10, 12$, with all trends continuing smoothly and
monotonically to the largest accessible exact-diagonalization system size. Extending both critical
points to $N=20$ via DMRG reveals a genuine quantitative distinction between the two universality
classes, with the XXZ critical point's finite-size scaling bearing the specific signature of the
marginally irrelevant operator known to exist at the SU(2) Heisenberg point, and the TFIM critical
point showing a clean power law consistent with the absence of such an operator at the Ising point.
